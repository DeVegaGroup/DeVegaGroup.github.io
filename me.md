# Dr Jose De Vega

I am a Group Leader (tenure track) at the Earlham Institute, Norwich. We use genomics and data-driven biology to investigate genome evolution in crops and agronomically important genetic diversity. My research sits at the interface of genome evolution, quantitative genetics, and crop improvement, with a particular focus on hybrid, polyploid, and clonally propagated crops.

## Current role

Since June 2022, I have been Group Leader (tenure track) at the Earlham Institute. Before that, from September 2017 to June 2022, I was Group Leader on a fellowship focused on genomics to improve crop tolerance to climate change within the Global Challenges Research Fund GROW Colombia project.

At Earlham, my group studies how hybridisation, polyploidisation, introgression, structural variation, and other complex forms of genomic diversity shape agronomic traits and how this knowledge can be translated into breeding. The overall aim is to understand the mechanistic basis of phenotypic variation, predict phenotypes, and work with breeders and other partners to accelerate crop improvement.

## Research and grants

My research programme combines population genomics, pangenomics, genome-wide association analyses, quantitative genetics, and long-read sequencing to study crop diversity and support breeding. It has been supported through competitive funding from UKRI, BBSRC, Horizon Europe, Innovate UK, ARIA, the British Council, the Royal Academy of Engineering, and other international programmes.

Current and recent grants include:
- **PI**: *Building environmental resilience through the lens of genomic technologies* (ARIA, 2025–2026)
- **PI**: Knowledge Transfer Partnership with Tozer Seeds to advance and integrate genomics technologies to accelerate the speed and efficiency of vegetable breeding (Innovate UK, 2024–2026)
- **Co-I, WP lead**: *Legume Generation: Boosting innovation in breeding for the next generation of legume crops for Europe* (Horizon Europe, 2023–2027)
- **Co-I, WP lead**: BBSRC Institute Strategic Programme *Decoding Biodiversity (DECODE)* (2023–2028)
- **Co-I**: BBSRC Institute Strategic Programme *Cellular Genomics (CELLGEN)* (2023–2028)
- **PI / CASE supervisor**: PhD projects in white clover, banana, and common bean
- **PI and Co-I** on earlier projects spanning crop biodiversity, digital infrastructure, forage genomics, rice genomics, sugarcane breeding, and biodiversity-based bioeconomy initiatives in Colombia, Peru, Vietnam, Kenya, and the UK

Across these projects, I have developed a research portfolio that links fundamental questions in crop genome evolution with translational outcomes in breeding, genomic resources, and phenotype prediction.

My recent invited talks and presentations have focused on crop diversity and evolution, pangenomic tools and workflows, legume and forage genomics, adaptation and admixture in common bean, and the application of genomics to crop resilience and net zero agriculture. ## Mentorship and supervision

Mentorship is a central part of my role. I have supervised two PhD students to completion, both currently pending minor corrections, and I am primary supervisor for two further PhD students. My students have shown consistent progress and increasing independence, supported by weekly one-to-one meetings, structured training, and opportunities for conference presentations, skills development, networking, and career planning.

I also serve as secondary supervisor for four PhD students, three at EI and one at the School of Biological Sciences, providing scientific direction, troubleshooting, and career development support.

I currently supervise three Post-Doctoral Research Associates, two of whom I recruited into their first independent research roles. All three have taken clear ownership of their projects and are progressing well. I actively support their transition towards independence by expanding their responsibilities, for example by serving as the lab’s Data Champion, leading internal coordination, or acting as the first point of contact for key collaborators, while ensuring that they receive advanced training, visibility opportunities, and support to present their work at conferences. I am also supporting the development of a future fellowship application for one of my PDRAs seeking independent funding. Former PDRAs from my group are now thriving as core service bioinformaticians at RBG Kew and EI.

During the first two years of my tenure track, I also supervised and mentored a recent graduate as a research assistant, providing structured guidance and research training. She subsequently began a PhD at Edinburgh in October 2023. Each year of my track, I have secured funding for and supervised summer interns. Feedback from interns has been consistently positive, and all have expressed a strong interest in pursuing research careers. Outcomes include one former intern now undertaking a PhD at Oxford and two who have applied to EI’s PhD programme this year, including one in my group. I treat internships not too differently from PhDs, providing interesting projects and creating opportunities for students to communicate their outputs.

Alongside direct supervision, I contribute to broader training and capacity-building by supporting senior postdocs in other groups and colleagues with funding applications for career transitions, including New Investigator applications, BBSRC fellowship bids, and PhD drafts in Plant Genomics. Externally, I have sponsored two applications from researchers in Sub-Saharan Africa to Royal Society schemes, including one individual now a Group Leader at ILRI.
