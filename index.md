# Decoding the Genomes of Complex Crops
One of the central questions in biological research is how phenotypes arise. Our group works at the interface of **genome evolution** and **crop improvement**. We investigate the mechanistic basis of phenotypic variation—looking at QTLs, gene expression, and epivariants—to understand how diversity can be leveraged for sustainable agriculture.

## The Challenge of Polyploidy
Many of the world's major crops are polyploid or of hybrid origin. Our research focuses on these complex allopolyploid systems, such as bananas and forage grasses, as well as species with multiple domestication gene pools like rice and beans. We study how chromosomal duplication buffers meiotic irregularities and how regulatory mismatches between subgenomes contribute to heterosis and adaptation.

## From Cataloging to Predicting
Our goal is not simply to "catalogue" diversity, but to understand its effects well enough to deliver tangible outcomes in breeding. By aligning with the Earlham Institute’s vision of predictive biology, we aim to simulate how genomes will behave under changing climates. 

## Vision for Sustainable Agrifood
We are committed to the idea that fundamental genomic insights must translate into impact. Whether it is improving the resilience of orphan crops or optimizing the yields of major legumes, our work is grounded in the necessity of securing the global food supply through data-driven biology.
